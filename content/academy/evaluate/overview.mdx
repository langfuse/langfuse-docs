# Evaluate

## What this is

Evaluation is the decision step of the loop. After you run an experiment, you need to decide whether the candidate system is actually better than the baseline and whether the evidence is strong enough to justify shipping it.

That is broader than scoring. A score is an input to evaluation. Evaluation is the act of interpreting evidence and making a decision.

## Why it matters

Without a clear evaluation step, teams either ship too eagerly based on a handful of nice examples or become paralyzed because every tradeoff feels debatable. Evaluation is what turns experimental output into an explicit decision.

It also forces you to confront the real tradeoffs. A change can improve answer quality while increasing cost. It can reduce latency while hurting edge-case coverage. Evaluation is where you decide which tradeoffs are acceptable.

## When to use it

Use the evaluate step when you have experiment results and need to answer questions like:

- Did the candidate actually win on the cases we care about?
- Is the quality gain large enough to justify the cost or latency increase?
- Is the result robust or driven by a few cherry-picked wins?
- Are failures concentrated in a high-risk segment even if the average score improved?
- Do we ship, iterate again, or gather more evidence?

## Core mental model

Evaluation compares evidence across multiple dimensions and ends with one of three outcomes:

| Outcome | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Ship | The candidate is better enough, with acceptable tradeoffs |
| Iterate | The change is promising, but the result is mixed or incomplete |
| Stop | The candidate is worse, too risky, or not worth the tradeoff |

### A meaningful win has to be defined, not assumed

Before running the experiment, decide what would count as success. That might include:

- A minimum quality improvement on key cases
- No regression on a defined high-risk slice
- Cost or latency staying within a threshold
- Fewer severe failures even if the average score changes only slightly

### Mixed evidence is normal

Experiment results are rarely clean. You may see:

- Better quality but higher cost
- Better average scores but worse performance on one important segment
- Faster latency but more brittle outputs
- Strong manual preference but noisy automated metrics

The job of evaluation is to interpret that evidence honestly instead of collapsing it into a single magic number.

## What good looks like

A good evaluation step usually has these properties:

- Success criteria were defined before the comparison was run.
- Quality, cost, latency, and risk are weighed explicitly instead of implicitly.
- Important slices are reviewed separately instead of hidden inside one average.
- Manual review is used to sanity-check automated scores on important cases.
- Inconclusive outcomes are allowed instead of forced into a yes or no.

## Common mistakes

- Treating one score as the full truth.
- Declaring a win without defining the decision rule in advance.
- Ignoring regressions in a small but critical class of cases.
- Shipping based on a few persuasive anecdotes.
- Forcing an inconclusive result into a confident decision.

## How it connects to the loop

Experiments generate the evidence that evaluation consumes. Evaluation then decides whether the candidate should ship. Once a change goes live, monitoring tells you whether the expected improvement holds up in production.

The evaluation methods themselves, such as manual review, code-based checks, and LLM-as-a-judge, are covered separately because they are used across multiple loop steps.

# Monitoring

## What this is

Monitoring is how you make sense of traced production behavior over time. It turns a stream of individual traces into trends, filters, alerts, and queues of cases that deserve attention.

The goal is not to stare at dashboards all day. The goal is to know when the system is drifting, getting expensive, slowing down, or failing users in ways worth acting on.

## Why it matters

Without monitoring, teams lurch between anecdotes and outages. One customer complaint feels existential. A quiet degradation goes unnoticed for weeks. Costs creep up without anyone realizing which feature or model is driving it.

Monitoring gives you two things the rest of the loop needs: a stable sense of what normal looks like and a way to surface cases that should influence future datasets or experiments.

## When to use it

Use monitoring when you need to answer questions like:

- Are quality signals drifting over time?
- Which traces should we inspect first?
- Are costs or latency increasing for a specific workflow?
- Did a recent change improve or damage live behavior?
- Are failures concentrated in a specific model, route, or user segment?

If tracing captures the evidence, monitoring decides what deserves ongoing attention.

## Core mental model

Monitoring has two jobs.

### 1. Track trends

Trend monitoring tells you whether system behavior is moving in the wrong direction over time. That includes cost, latency, failures, and quality signals.

### 2. Surface cases

Case monitoring tells you which specific traces are worth looking at right now. A trend tells you that something changed. A surfaced case tells you where to start.

### The signal categories that matter

| Category | Tracking | Detection |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Quality | Judge scores, code-based checks, user feedback, correction rates | Low-scoring traces, policy breaks, disagreement, repeated fallback behavior |
| Cost | Total spend, cost per feature, cost per model, token use | Expensive outlier traces, runaway loops, unusually large prompts |
| Latency | p50, p95, p99 latency, step-level delays | Timeouts, slow tool chains, slow retrieval or generation steps |
| Reliability | Error rates, retry rates, abandonment | Broken tools, malformed outputs, repeated failures in one path |

## What good looks like

A good monitoring setup usually has these properties:

- A small set of primary signals tied to real user or business risk.
- Trends are segmented by route, model, feature, or user cohort when that distinction matters.
- Alerts or saved filters lead back to concrete traces, not just summary charts.
- Quality monitoring is specific enough to be actionable, not a vague “helpfulness” number with no interpretation path.
- Live evaluators are sampled intentionally when they are expensive, rather than run blindly on everything.

## Common mistakes

- Monitoring only cost and latency while ignoring quality.
- Creating dashboards that show problems but do not lead to concrete traces.
- Using generic scores with no clear connection to real failure modes.
- Running expensive evaluators on every trace before proving they are worth the cost.
- Treating monitoring as the place to make shipping decisions instead of the place to identify what needs follow-up.

## How it connects to the loop

Tracing provides the raw material monitoring depends on. Monitoring then tells you which cases should become part of a dataset and which issues are serious enough to justify a structured experiment.

Monitoring is about prioritization. It tells you what deserves to become repeatable learning, not just what looked strange once.

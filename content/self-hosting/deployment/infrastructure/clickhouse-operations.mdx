# ClickHouse operations playbook

<Callout type="info">

This is the day-2 operations companion to the [ClickHouse infrastructure guide](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse), which covers setup, environment variables, and deployment options in detail.

</Callout>

ClickHouse is the OLAP database that stores all Langfuse tracing data, and on self-hosted deployments it is usually the component that needs the most operational attention.
Most ClickHouse incidents we see on self-hosted Langfuse fall into three buckets: disk growth (typically system log tables or missing data retention), part accumulation and merge pressure, and instances that are undersized for their read workload.
This playbook covers how Langfuse uses ClickHouse, how to size, monitor, and back it up, and how to troubleshoot the failure patterns that come up most often.
If you do not want to own these operations, [ClickHouse Cloud or BYOC](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse#cloudbyoc) removes most of them.

## How Langfuse uses ClickHouse [#how-langfuse-uses-clickhouse]

Langfuse stores traces, observations, and scores in ClickHouse, alongside auxiliary tables such as `event_log` and `blob_storage_file_log`.
Understanding the write and read paths helps you interpret every metric and failure mode in this guide:

- **All tracing tables use the [ReplacingMergeTree](https://clickhouse.com/docs/engines/table-engines/mergetree-family/replacingmergetree) engine.** Updates are written as new row versions with the same sorting key, and ClickHouse deduplicates them during background merges. Langfuse additionally deduplicates at read time (for example with the `FINAL` modifier) because background merges are eventual.
- **Inserts are batched by design.** Incoming events are written to S3/blob storage first and queued in Redis, then the Langfuse worker accumulates them in an in-memory buffer and flushes them to ClickHouse in batches, with `async_inserts` enabled on the ClickHouse side. Large, infrequent inserts are the access pattern ClickHouse is built for, and Langfuse follows it.
- **Reads are optimized for project and time filters.** Tracing tables are ordered and partitioned around `project_id` and time (monthly partitions), so queries without a time filter must scan far more data.
- **Deletes are mutations.** Data retention jobs and trace deletions rewrite affected parts in the background, which temporarily increases disk usage and merge activity.

Langfuse supports ClickHouse versions >= 24.3, and the server [must run in UTC](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse#timezones).

<Callout type="info">

As of July 2026, Langfuse Cloud is rolling out an observation-centric data model on a new, mostly immutable events table ([Fast Preview](/docs/v4)). Self-hosted deployments currently run the v3 data model described above; the OSS migration path is announced in the [changelog](/changelog) and the [technical blog post](/blog/2026-03-10-simplify-langfuse-for-scale) when available.

</Callout>

## Choosing a deployment option [#choosing-a-deployment-option]

For larger production deployments we recommend ClickHouse Cloud or BYOC, because they remove most of the disk, merge, and replica management covered in this guide; self-managed ClickHouse is a good fit when your team can own those operations.
See the [support levels](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse#support) for the official list; the table below compares the options from an operations perspective.

| Option                                                                                                             | Support level | You operate                                             | Best fit                                                                    |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| [ClickHouse Cloud](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse#cloudbyoc)                                   | Official      | Nothing (managed service)                               | Teams that want managed scaling, backups, and storage-compute separation    |
| [ClickHouse BYOC](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse#cloudbyoc)                                    | Official      | Cloud account only (data plane in your VPC)             | Large deployments with data residency or VPC-boundary requirements          |
| [ClickHouse Kubernetes Operator](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse#clickhouse-kubernetes-operator) | Official      | Disks, merges, upgrades, backups, Keeper                | Teams with ClickHouse experience that need full control on Kubernetes       |
| [Bitnami Helm chart](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse#clickhouse-on-kubernetes-helm)             | Community     | Disks, merges, upgrades, backups, Keeper                | Default dependency of [Langfuse K8s](https://github.com/langfuse/langfuse-k8s) |
| [Single-container Docker](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse#docker)                               | Community     | Everything, with no redundancy                          | Development and testing only                                                |

Two structural differences matter most for operations:

- **Storage-compute separation.** ClickHouse Cloud and BYOC use [SharedMergeTree](https://clickhouse.com/docs/cloud/reference/shared-merge-tree), so storage grows independently of compute and there is no volume expansion or shard capacity planning. Self-managed OSS ClickHouse ties storage to each replica's disk unless you configure [blob storage as disk](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse#blob-storage-as-disk).
- **Workload isolation.** Cloud and BYOC support compute-compute separation through [warehouses](https://clickhouse.com/docs/cloud/reference/warehouses), which Langfuse can use via [`CLICKHOUSE_READ_ONLY_URL`](/self-hosting/configuration/scaling#clickhouse-read-only-url) to keep read traffic off the ingestion path. On self-managed setups, all workloads share the same replicas.

## Sizing [#sizing]

Start with the documented minimum of 2 CPUs and 8 GiB of memory for ClickHouse, plan at least 16 GiB of memory for larger deployments, and scale vertically before anything else.
Langfuse [does not support multi-shard ClickHouse clusters](#faq-sharding), so capacity planning is about one shard: bigger replicas and more disk, not more shards.
A single ClickHouse shard can handle multiple terabytes of Langfuse data.

There is no universal events-per-day sizing table because resource needs depend on the shape of your workload. Size along these dimensions and iterate based on the [monitoring signals](#monitoring) below:

- **Ingestion volume** drives CPU for inserts and background merges: observations per day and peak bursts matter more than averages.
- **Payload size** drives disk and merge cost: LLM inputs, outputs, and metadata dominate row size, and truncating or trimming them client-side has outsized effects.
- **Retention window** drives total disk: stored bytes scale roughly linearly with retention days at constant ingestion.
- **Read concurrency** drives memory: dashboards, the Metrics and Observations APIs, and evaluators all run analytical queries, and ClickHouse rewards memory with faster responses.
- **Update and delete rate** drives merge and mutation load: retention jobs, trace deletions, and non-OTel SDKs that update rows all create background work.

Practical starting points from the deployment docs: use at least the `large` resources preset and 3 replicas on the Helm chart, start with a 100 Gi volume on a storage class that allows expansion, and grow memory first when UI or API queries get slow (see the [scaling guide](/self-hosting/configuration/scaling)).

## Disk management [#disk-management]

Configure [Langfuse data retention](/docs/administration/data-retention) first and cap the ClickHouse system log tables second; together these two steps resolve the large majority of disk problems on self-hosted deployments.

### Find out what is using disk [#disk-usage-breakdown]

Run this query to identify the largest tables, including ClickHouse's own system tables:

```sql
SELECT table, formatReadableSize(size) as size, rows FROM (
    SELECT
        table,
        database,
        sum(bytes) AS size,
        sum(rows) AS rows
    FROM system.parts
    WHERE active
    GROUP BY table, database
    ORDER BY size DESC
)
```

If system tables such as `trace_log` or `metric_log` top the list, fix those first; if Langfuse tables top the list, work through retention and TTLs.

### Delete old tracing data [#delete-old-data]

Langfuse's built-in [Data Retention](/docs/administration/data-retention) feature deletes traces, observations, scores, and media assets older than a configured number of days, nightly, across both ClickHouse and blob storage.
If data retention is not available on your plan, you can attach ClickHouse [TTLs](https://clickhouse.com/docs/guides/developer/ttl) to the `traces`, `observations`, `scores`, and `event_log` tables instead.

### Cap the ClickHouse system log tables [#system-log-tables]

On default configurations, ClickHouse's system log tables (`trace_log`, `text_log`, `opentelemetry_span_log`, `asynchronous_metric_log`, `metric_log`, `latency_log`) ship without a TTL and can grow to dominate disk usage.
Langfuse does not read from these tables, so you can disable the unused ones or attach short TTLs.
See [ClickHouse system log tables](/self-hosting/configuration/scaling#clickhouse-system-log-tables) for ready-to-use `config.d` snippets and the retrofit steps for existing installs.

### Why disk does not shrink immediately [#inactive-parts]

Freeing disk in ClickHouse is asynchronous: deletes are mutations that rewrite parts in the background, and merged or mutated parts stay on disk as inactive parts for a grace period (controlled by the `old_parts_lifetime` MergeTree setting) before they are removed.
Expect disk usage to rise temporarily during large retention runs or deletions before it falls.
You can measure how much space inactive parts hold:

```sql
SELECT
    table,
    count() AS inactive_parts,
    formatReadableSize(sum(bytes_on_disk)) AS size
FROM system.parts
WHERE NOT active
GROUP BY table
ORDER BY sum(bytes_on_disk) DESC
```

A persistently large volume of inactive parts on the `observations` or `traces` table indicates that merges or mutations cannot keep up; see [troubleshooting](#troubleshooting-inactive-parts) below.

### Tier or offload storage [#storage-tiering]

For deployments where local volumes become the bottleneck, ClickHouse supports [object storage as a disk](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse#blob-storage-as-disk), which moves data to S3, Azure Blob Storage, or GCS while keeping metadata on a local volume.
On top of a multi-disk storage configuration, ClickHouse `TTL ... TO DISK` and `TTL ... TO VOLUME` clauses can implement hot/cold tiering, moving older partitions to cheaper storage instead of deleting them; see the [ClickHouse external disks documentation](https://clickhouse.com/docs/en/operations/storing-data) for the mechanics.
Mind the bucket configuration warnings on the infrastructure page: no bucket versioning and no deletion lifecycle policies on ClickHouse-managed buckets.

### Expand volumes [#expand-volumes]

On Kubernetes, expand the ClickHouse PVCs and restart the StatefulSet; the [NOT_ENOUGH_SPACE troubleshooting section](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse#clickhouse-on-kubernetes-helm) has the exact commands.
Use a storage class with `allowVolumeExpansion: true` and alert at 80% disk capacity so expansion happens before writes fail.

## Replication and high availability [#replication-and-ha]

Run at least 3 replicas for production self-managed ClickHouse; Langfuse fully supports replicated setups but does not support sharding.

- **Replication is handled through ClickHouse's replicated table engines.** With `CLICKHOUSE_CLUSTER_ENABLED=true` (the default), Langfuse runs its migrations `ON CLUSTER` so tables are created as replicated tables across all nodes; the required `macros` and `default_replica_*` settings are covered by the Helm chart and the operator.
- **A replica count of 1 means no redundancy.** ClickHouse counts every instance toward the replica count, and the number of replicas cannot be increased at runtime without manual intervention or downtime, so provision 3 replicas from the start.
- **Keeper is part of your availability story.** Replication coordination runs through ClickHouse Keeper or ZooKeeper; include its volumes in [backups](/self-hosting/configuration/backups) and monitor it like any other stateful component.
- **Sharding is not supported.** Set `shards: 1`; if you approach the capacity of a single shard, consider [ClickHouse Cloud or BYOC](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse#cloudbyoc), where SharedMergeTree replicas share object storage and scale without shard planning.

## Monitoring and alerting [#monitoring]

Watch five signals on a self-hosted ClickHouse: disk usage, parts per partition, merge backlog, replication lag, and Langfuse's ingestion queue length.
ClickHouse system tables are per-node, so run the queries below against each replica (or aggregate them via the `clusterAllReplicas` table function).

| Signal            | Source                                                     | Alert when                                            |
| ----------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| Disk usage        | `system.disks`, volume metrics                             | Above 80% capacity                                    |
| Parts count       | `system.parts`                                             | Sustained growth, or "Too many parts" insert errors   |
| Merge backlog     | `system.merges`                                            | Merges continuously running without draining          |
| Replication lag   | `system.replicas`                                          | `absolute_delay` rising over minutes                  |
| Ingestion queue   | `langfuse.queue.ingestion.length` (statsd)                 | Queue length growing steadily                         |

```sql
-- Disk headroom per configured disk
SELECT name, formatReadableSize(free_space) AS free, formatReadableSize(total_space) AS total
FROM system.disks;

-- Active parts per partition (high counts signal merge pressure)
SELECT database, table, partition, count() AS parts
FROM system.parts
WHERE active
GROUP BY database, table, partition
ORDER BY parts DESC
LIMIT 10;

-- Currently running merges
SELECT database, table, elapsed, round(progress, 2) AS progress,
       formatReadableSize(total_size_bytes_compressed) AS size
FROM system.merges;

-- Replication lag and queue per table
SELECT database, table, absolute_delay, queue_size
FROM system.replicas
ORDER BY absolute_delay DESC;
```

On the Langfuse side:

- The worker publishes queue length metrics via statsd; `langfuse.queue.ingestion.length` is the main scaling signal, and setting `ENABLE_AWS_CLOUDWATCH_METRIC_PUBLISHING=true` publishes it to CloudWatch. See the worker scaling section of the [scaling guide](/self-hosting/configuration/scaling).
- The Langfuse containers emit OpenTelemetry traces that you can send to your own collector via [`OTEL_EXPORTER_OTLP_ENDPOINT`](/self-hosting/configuration/observability).
- Check [background migrations](/self-hosting/upgrade/background-migrations) in the UI after every upgrade and alert on failed migrations.

On ClickHouse Cloud and BYOC, built-in monitoring and notifications cover the database-side signals, so you mainly keep the Langfuse-side metrics.

## Routine operations [#routine-operations]

### Upgrades [#upgrades]

Langfuse applies its ClickHouse schema migrations automatically on startup, and long-running data changes run as [background migrations](/self-hosting/upgrade/background-migrations) that you should verify after each upgrade.
As of July 2026, Langfuse does not require deployment stops between minor releases; required stops are highlighted in the changelog.
For ClickHouse server upgrades on self-managed clusters, upgrade replicas one at a time (a rolling upgrade) and stay within the supported range of versions >= 24.3; the [ClickHouse Kubernetes Operator](https://clickhouse.com/docs/clickhouse-operator/overview) automates upgrades for operator-managed clusters.

### Backups [#backups]

ClickHouse Cloud and BYOC back up automatically; self-managed deployments need their own strategy, covered in detail on the [backups page](/self-hosting/configuration/backups).
In short: use volume snapshots (including the Keeper/ZooKeeper volumes), Velero for whole-namespace backups on Kubernetes, or ClickHouse-native `BACKUP`/`RESTORE` to object storage.
Test restores regularly so the procedure is proven before you depend on it.

### Migrating between ClickHouse instances [#migrating-clusters]

To move Langfuse to a different ClickHouse instance (for example from a single node to a replicated cluster, or from self-managed to ClickHouse Cloud), point Langfuse at the new instance first so new data lands there, then back up the old instance to blob storage and restore it into the new one.
The [migration section of the infrastructure guide](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse#migrating-to-a-new-instance) links the ClickHouse-provided guides for `remoteSecure` and backup/restore paths.
A concurrent dual-write setup is technically possible but untested and not recommended by ClickHouse; reach out to Langfuse support if you want to explore it.

## Troubleshooting [#troubleshooting]

### Disk keeps growing despite data retention [#troubleshooting-disk-growth]

Check the ClickHouse system log tables first; they have no TTL by default and can grow to dominate disk usage on busy instances.
Run the [largest-tables query](#disk-usage-breakdown), then apply the fixes in [ClickHouse system log tables](/self-hosting/configuration/scaling#clickhouse-system-log-tables).
If Langfuse tables dominate instead, verify the retention period matches your expectations and remember that [deletes free space asynchronously](#inactive-parts).

### Inactive parts accumulate on large tables [#troubleshooting-inactive-parts]

Tens or hundreds of GiB of inactive parts on the `observations` or `traces` table means old parts are being produced faster than ClickHouse removes them, which points to merge or mutation pressure rather than a leak.
Work through these steps:

1. Check `system.merges` and `system.mutations` for long-running or stuck entries; slow storage throughput is a common bottleneck for merges.
2. Reduce mutation pressure from deletions: on ClickHouse 25.7+, set `CLICKHOUSE_LIGHTWEIGHT_DELETE_MODE=lightweight_update` and `CLICKHOUSE_USE_LIGHTWEIGHT_UPDATE=true` so Langfuse uses lightweight deletes and updates instead of `ALTER TABLE` mutations (see [deletion tuning](/self-hosting/configuration/scaling#clickhouse-deletion-timeouts)).
3. Give the instance more headroom: merges compete with inserts and queries for CPU, memory, and disk I/O, and vertical scaling is the intended remedy on a single-shard setup.
4. Size volumes with slack: transient inactive-part spikes during retention runs are expected, so alerting at 80% capacity leaves room for them.

### "Too many parts" errors or merge backlog [#troubleshooting-too-many-parts]

ClickHouse throws "Too many parts" when inserts create new parts faster than background merges consolidate them, and the fix is fewer, larger inserts plus enough merge throughput.
Langfuse already batches its inserts through the worker and `async_inserts`, so on a Langfuse-only instance this usually indicates an undersized instance or slow disks rather than an application problem.
If other tools write to the same ClickHouse (custom pipelines, log shippers), verify they batch their inserts too, and keep heavy ad-hoc analytics off the ingestion instance per the [direct access guidance](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse#direct-clickhouse-access).

### Retention jobs or deletions time out [#troubleshooting-deletion-timeouts]

Increase `LANGFUSE_CLICKHOUSE_DELETION_TIMEOUT_MS` (default 10 minutes) to give long-running deletes more headroom; see [slow or timing-out deletions](/self-hosting/configuration/scaling#clickhouse-deletion-timeouts).
If the retention job times out on blob storage cleanup, the [`blob_storage_file_log` FAQ](/faq/all/data-retention-timeouts-and-errors) describes when you can skip that step.

### Migration fails with "Dirty database version" [#troubleshooting-dirty-migrations]

A failed ClickHouse migration marks the schema as dirty and blocks further migrations until you force the version to match the actual state.
Follow the [failed migrations FAQ](/faq/all/self-hosting-clickhouse-handling-failed-migrations) to inspect and resolve the state safely.

### UI or API queries are slow [#troubleshooting-slow-queries]

Slow dashboards and API responses usually mean missing project/time filters or an undersized ClickHouse; both causes and their fixes are covered in the [scaling guide](/self-hosting/configuration/scaling).

## FAQ [#faq]

### Should I use ClickHouse Cloud or self-manage ClickHouse for Langfuse? [#faq-cloud-vs-self-managed]

We recommend ClickHouse Cloud or BYOC for larger Langfuse deployments because storage-compute separation and warehouses remove disk planning, shard capacity limits, and most of the operations in this guide.
Self-managed ClickHouse (via the official Kubernetes operator or the Bitnami Helm chart) is a good fit for air-gapped environments, strict infrastructure policies, or teams that already operate ClickHouse.
BYOC sits in between: a managed control plane with the data plane inside your own cloud account.

### Does Langfuse support sharded ClickHouse clusters? [#faq-sharding]

No, Langfuse requires a single-shard ClickHouse cluster (`shards: 1`); replication across nodes is fully supported, sharding is not.
A single shard handles multiple terabytes of data, and deployments that outgrow it should consider ClickHouse Cloud or BYOC, where SharedMergeTree scales without shards.

### Does Langfuse work with ReplicatedMergeTree? [#faq-replicatedmergetree]

Yes, replicated setups are the recommended production configuration.
With `CLICKHOUSE_CLUSTER_ENABLED=true` (the default), Langfuse runs its migrations `ON CLUSTER` and creates its tables as replicated tables, using the `macros` and `default_replica_*` settings that the Helm chart and the ClickHouse operator provide.

### Can Langfuse share a ClickHouse cluster with other workloads? [#faq-shared-cluster]

Langfuse can run in its own database on an existing cluster (set `CLICKHOUSE_DB` and grant the [required permissions](/self-hosting/deployment/infrastructure/clickhouse#user-permissions)), but its ingestion is write-heavy and merge-intensive, so co-located workloads will compete for CPU, memory, and disk I/O.
Keep heavy analytical workloads separated from the Langfuse ingestion path, for example on a separate compute group on ClickHouse Cloud/BYOC.

### Why is disk usage still high after deleting data? [#faq-disk-after-delete]

Deletes in ClickHouse are asynchronous mutations, and replaced parts remain on disk as inactive parts for a grace period before removal, so space is reclaimed with a delay after a deletion, not immediately.
If usage stays high permanently, check the [system log tables](/self-hosting/configuration/scaling#clickhouse-system-log-tables) and the [inactive parts troubleshooting steps](#troubleshooting-inactive-parts).

### How do I move from self-managed ClickHouse to ClickHouse Cloud? [#faq-migrate-to-cloud]

Point Langfuse at the new ClickHouse Cloud service first so incoming data is captured there, then restore a backup of the old instance into it; the [migration section](#migrating-clusters) and the linked ClickHouse guides cover both the `remoteSecure` and backup/restore paths.
